# Rider Persona: Linda

## Background
- **Age**: Mid-50s
- **Experience**: 30+ years in the saddle
- **Disciplines**: USDF traditional dressage and Western Dressage
- **Level**: Showing Training through Second Level (traditional), Basic through Level 2 (Western)
- **Horses**: Owns two — a warmblood for traditional and a quarter horse for western
- **Setting**: Private barn on her property, outdoor 20×60m arena, no indoor

## Technology Profile
- iPhone user, comfortable with apps and Bluetooth devices
- Weekly virtual lessons via **Pivo camera** — already familiar with arena-mounted tech
- Rides with **wireless Beats headphones** — audio during rides is already normal for her
- Tech-tolerant but **zero patience for friction or failure**. If it doesn't work the first time, she's done with it.
- Will not fumble with her phone while mounted. Setup must happen before she gets on.

## Current Test Learning Workflow
- Prints tests from USDF website or uses a **paper test book**
- Studies the diagram and movement descriptions the night before
- Reads through the test once more on the mounting block
- Rides through from memory, sometimes blanking mid-test
- Occasionally asks her husband to stand at the rail and call, but he isn't always available and doesn't know the correct timing
- Her virtual coach sometimes calls during lessons, but audio lag and the coach being focused on training (not calling) makes it unreliable

## Pain Points
1. **No reliable caller for solo practice** — she rides alone 5–6 days a week
2. **Memorization stress** — she shows in both traditional and western dressage, so she may need 3–4 different tests in a show weekend. Mixing them up is a real fear.
3. **Paper is fragile** — test sheets blow away, get rained on, can't be read from the saddle
4. **Existing caller apps require manual interaction** — tapping or saying "next" breaks her concentration and isn't realistic practice
5. **Husband as caller** — well-meaning but reads too early, too late, or adds commentary. She wants consistency.

## What "Good Enough" Looks Like
- **As reliable as a paper test book** — if the paper test never crashes or loses Bluetooth, neither should this
- **Audio quality at least as good as her coach on a Zoom call** — clear, well-timed, no robotic weirdness
- **Setup in under 2 minutes** — she's already warming up in her head while tacking up. The app is not the main event.
- **Works with her existing Beats headphones** — no new audio hardware
- **Handles both traditional and western dressage tests** — she doesn't want two apps

## A Typical Practice Session (What the App Must Fit Into)
1. Tacks up horse (~15 min)
2. Sets up Pivo on tripod at arena center, starts recording
3. Walks to mounting block, gets on
4. Warms up 10–15 min (walk, trot, canter, stretching — no test work)
5. **Runs through test 1–2 times** ← this is where the app matters
6. Works on problem movements from the test
7. Cools down, dismounts
8. Total ride: 45–60 min, test run-throughs are maybe 15 min of that

## Her Expectations for the Beacons
- Willing to do a **one-time setup** if it's straightforward — attach beacons to letter posts, walk the perimeter once
- After that, beacons should just be there. She will not re-pair, re-calibrate, or troubleshoot before every ride.
- If a beacon battery dies, she wants to know which one and swap it in 30 seconds
- Beacons must survive Oklahoma weather: 105°F summers, ice storms, blowing dust

## Phrases She Would Use
- "I just want it to call the test like a person would."
- "If I have to look at my phone while I'm riding, it's useless."
- "My horse doesn't care about technology. I need to focus on him, not the app."
- "I already printed the test. Can this really be better than paper?"
